Data Privacy in the US

Data privacy is the idea that one should be allowed to determine for oneself what personal information is shared, to whom it is shared with, and to what extent that information is shared. This consists of requiring those on the receiving end of data transactions to only use the data for its intended purpose. The right to privacy is understood; no one wants a stranger to know their legal name, address, or their credit card number. When it comes to the selling of user data, though, this right is violated. Data privacy, just like the good old fashioned right to privacy, should be considered a human right.

In the United States, there have been many issues related to data privacy. One company, with many examples of these issues, is Facebook. An example of these issues is when "the personal information of over 533 million Facebook users from 106 countries, including over 32 million records on users in the US, 11 million on users in the UK, and 6 million on users in India" was published on a hacking forum, according to Business Insider. A CNN article on this data leak states that "names, phone numbers, Facebook IDs, locations, account creation dates, birthdays, relationship statuses, bios and, in some cases, email addresses" were leaked. This is an obvious issue.

Another issue is Facebook tracking data even if you don't have an account, or aren't logged in. According to Newsweek, this was confirmed on Monday, April 16, 2018, when the company was trying to "shake off the unprecedented controversy that arose following allegations that it facilitated a breach of user data linked to an estimated 87 million accounts". This data leak is separate from the one discussed earlier. In a blog post, David Baser, Facebook's product management director responded to the claim that Facebook tracks data of people who don't have an account, stating that Facebook is not the only company to do this. He mentioned Twitter, LinkedIn, Google, Amazon and Pinterest as examples. This does not make the issue with Facebook any smaller, only shows how widespread the issues with data privacy spread.

According to the United States Federal Trade Commission, "Facebook repeatedly used deceptive disclosures and settings to undermine users’ privacy preferences". The company had to pay a five billion dollar penalty. While it is good, this fine is currently one of a kind. It's obvious that people realize, due to the examples shown by Facebook, that data privacy laws in the US are an issue, so where is the legislation?

In Europe on the other hand, there has been legislation to protect user data, specifically, the General Data Protection Regulation. According to Brink News, "The impact of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has been widespread. Europe’s new data protection laws have resulted in 281,000 breaches and 55 million euros ($61 million) in fines to some of the world’s biggest tech companies over the mishandling of personal information. Furthermore, the legislation has drawn a line in the sand as to what companies can and cannot do when it comes to sensitive user data." While the GDPR isn't clear on everything, it is a large step above the current situation in the US, as there is no federal legislation on the topic.

Yet the individual states don't have the legislation needed either. According to CIO Dive, in the year following the GDPR, there was an uptick in the number of GDPR-like bills being created in individual states, but "year two shows no indication regulations will be up and running". Less than 20 states had legislation in the making, one being California, with the California Consumer Protection Act (CCPA), which has set a precedent for this kind of legislation in the states.

The US needs stricter Data Privacy laws and needs to consider Data Privacy a Human Right. According to a study by KPMG, one of the largest professional service companies in the world, "89% of consumers say data privacy is a human right" and that nine in every ten Americans believes that data privacy is a human right. The people of the US believe that their data should be protected. We need to support bills inspired by the GDPR and the CCPA, such as the Alabama Consumer Privacy Act, and the Massachusetts Information Privacy Act, and we need to build a world where data privacy is valued by large companies just as much as it is valued by individuals.
